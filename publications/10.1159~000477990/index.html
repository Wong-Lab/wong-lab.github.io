A Role for Neuronal Alpha-Synuclein in Gastrointestinal Immunity

Ethan StolzenbergDeborah BerryDe YangErnest Y. LeeAlexander KroemerStuart KaufmanGerard C.L. WongJoost J. OppenheimSupti SenThomas FishbeinAd BaxBrent HarrisDenise BarbutMichael A. Zasloff
Published on 2017 Â· Journal of Innate Immunity Â· PDF

Abstract#

Background: Alpha-synuclein (Î±S) is a nerve cell protein associated with Parkinson disease (PD). Accumulation of Î±S within the enteric nervous system (ENS) and its traffic from the gut to the brain are implicated in the pathogenesis and progression of PD. Î±S has no known function in humans and the reason for its accumulation within the ENS is unknown. Several recent studies conducted in rodents have linked Î±S to immune cell activation in the central nervous system. We hypothesized that Î±S in the ENS might play a role in the innate immune defenses of the human gastrointestinal (GI) tract. Methods: We immunostained endoscopic biopsies for Î±S from children with documented gastric and duodenal inflammation and intestinal allograft recipients who contracted norovirus. To determine whether Î±S exhibited immune-modulatory activity, we examined whether human Î±S induced leukocyte migration and dendritic cell maturation. Findings: We showed that the expression of Î±S in the enteric neurites of the upper GI tract of pediatric patients positively correlated with the degree of acute and chronic inflammation in the intestinal wall. In intestinal allograft subjects who were closely monitored for infection, expression of Î±S was induced during norovirus infection. We also demonstrated that both monomeric and oligomeric Î±S have potent chemoattractant activity, causing the migration of neutrophils and monocytes dependent on the presence of the integrin subunit, CD11b, and that both forms of Î±S stimulate dendritic cell maturation. Interpretation: These findings strongly suggest that Î±S is expressed within the human ENS to direct intestinal inflammation and implicates common GI infections in the pathogenesis of PD.
